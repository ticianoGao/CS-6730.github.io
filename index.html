What is this project about?

Digital devices have become a necessity in daily life, shaping how people spend time and access information. Smartphones enable people to access information quickly and comfortably. However, excessive reliance on these devices negatively affects our mental and physical health. Existing literature presents connections between varying aspects of health and smartphone and social media usage. Furthermore, these relationships tend to vary across different age groups (Vettriselvan et al., 2025). Our project draws mainly from the Digital Lifestyle Benchmark Dataset from Kaggle.


This dataset includes health condition variables (e.g., depression score, anxiety score) and digital device usage proxies (e.g., social media minutes, digital dependence score) of 3,500 individuals.


We are interested in exploring the relationship between digital device usage and mental wellbeing. Our objective is to draw insights from the data and determine how we might adjust our own screen time to support better wellbeing and overall health.

Dataset at a glance

This section presents an overview of the dataset, focusing on sleep quality and age distributions.

Overall Sleep Quality Distribution

The dataset for this analysis is based on survey responses capturing individuals’ self-reported digital device usage alongside measures of mental wellbeing. It includes 3,500 participants. A majority of respondents (58%) report experiencing poor or very poor sleep quality, while 42% indicate they achieve good or excellent sleep. However, only 14% report excellent sleep quality. This finding underscores the importance of understanding how digital device usage may be associated with mental wellbeing.

Respondent Distribution by Age

The sample is predominantly composed of younger individuals, with 58% of respondents aged 20–34. The largest proportion of respondents falls within the 20–25 age group.


We find that ages 50–54 represent only 1% of the sample, so findings for this group should be interpreted with caution.



Does Longer Screen Time Improve Happiness and Mental Wellbeing?

We use a heatmap to visualize digital device usage and mental health scores across age groups. The first key insight is that digital usage declines with age, with younger individuals showing significantly higher engagement with digital devices.


Happiness scores remain relatively stable across different age groups (excluding ages 50-54).


Depression and anxiety scores are particularly high among the youngest individuals (ages 10–14). This may suggest that younger teens’ mental wellbeing is more affected by longer screen time.


An interesting insight is that the 50–54 age group appears counterintuitive. Despite relatively high screen time, they show lower engagement with social media. At the same time, they report the lowest happiness scores and the highest levels of depression and anxiety. However, this finding should be interpreted with caution, as this age group represents only 1% of the data.

How digital exposure affects mental strain across education levels

These visualizations compare how notification frequency, device usage, sleep risk, focus risk, and stress vary across education groups. Together, they highlight how digital interruptions consistently influence mental strain regardless of academic background.

Mental Strain vs Notification Frequency

The heatmap confirms that higher notification levels are associated with higher mental strain across education groups.
