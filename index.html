Project Overview

What is this project about?

This project investigates possible relationships between digital device usage and mental health-related factors. Through a series of interactive visualizations, we examine patterns in sleep quality, age distribution, focus score, and well-being to better understand how digital lifestyle may influence everyday life.

Overall Sleep Quality Distribution

The dataset for this analysis is based on survey responses capturing individuals’ self-reported digital device usage alongside measures of mental wellbeing. It includes 3,500 participants. A majority of respondents (58%) report experiencing poor or very poor sleep quality, while 42% indicate they achieve good or excellent sleep. However, only 14% report excellent sleep quality. This finding underscores the importance of understanding how digital device usage may be associated with mental wellbeing.

Respondent Distribution by Age

The sample is predominantly composed of younger individuals, with 58% of respondents aged 20–34. The largest proportion of respondents falls within the 20–25 age group.

Age and Well-Being

Digital device usage and average well-being scores by age group

We use a heatmap to visualize digital device usage and mental health scores across age groups. The first key insight is that digital usage declines with age, with younger individuals showing significantly higher engagement with digital devices.


Happiness scores remain relatively stable across different age groups (excluding ages 50-54).


Depression and anxiety scores are particularly high among the youngest individuals (ages 10–14). This may suggest that younger teens’ mental wellbeing is more affected by longer screen time.


An interesting insight is that the 50–54 age group appears counterintuitive. Despite relatively high screen time, they show lower engagement with social media. At the same time, they report the lowest happiness scores and the highest levels of depression and anxiety. However, this finding should be interpreted with caution, as this age group represents only 1% of the data.
